From the Fourier series to the Fourier transform

Signal Processing

How to go from the Fourier series to the Fourier transform.

Published

February 3, 2026

Introduction

  • The Fourier transform blog post gives the definition of the Fourier transform.

  • But it does not explain where it comes from.

  • This blog post explains where the Fourier transform comes from.

Summary

The post is organized as follows

  • The Fourier series section explains that

    • Fourier had the intuition (but no proof) for the following equality for periodic signals (see Fourier’s intuition)

      \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}cos(2\pi n\xi t) + b_{n}sin(2\pi n\xi t) \right\rbrack\) (1)

  • This expression can be rewritten as (see From sine/cosine to complex exponentials)

    \(f(t) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi n\xi t}\) (2)

  • Which makes

    • Complex exponentials appear.

    • Positive and negative frequencies appear.

  • The corresponding Fourier series coefficients can be derived from orthogonality as (see From Fourier series intuition to Fourier series coefficients)

    \(c_{n} = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi n\xi t}dt\)

  • The From the Fourier Series to the Fourier Transform section explains that

    • The Fourier series intuition can be extended to a non-periodic signal by seeing it as the limit of a periodic signal as its period \(T \rightarrow \infty\).

    • This leads to (see From the Fourier Series to the Fourier Transform)

      \(f(t) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi\) (3)

  • Note that even though expression (3) is the limit of the Fourier series (expression (2)) as \(T \rightarrow \infty\), it is not called the Fourier transform.

    • In fact, it is the inverse Fourier transform instead.

    • The Fourier transform is the function \(F(\xi)\) of \(\xi\) (i.e. the Fourier transform coefficients).

  • Similarly to the Fourier series, this leads to the Fourier transform coefficients

    \(F(\xi) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi\xi t}dt\)

Fourier series

  • This section explains Fourier's intuition that led to the Fourier series and the Fourier series coefficients.

Fourier’s intuition

  • Fourier had the intuition that any periodic signal with period \(T\) (or frequency \(\xi = \frac{1}{T}\)) can be decomposed as an infinite sum of oscillations at frequencies that are integer multiples of \(\xi\).

  • He did not prove this intuition.

  • He used it as an assumption and derived the Fourier series coefficients from this assumption.

  • The empirical success suggested that the assumption was correct.

  • But mathematical proof came later.

    • Dirichlet provided the first rigorous proof in 1829, valid under certain conditions now called the Dirichlet conditions.

    • This proof is only valid for Fourier series / periodic signals.

    • Hence, this proof does not apply to modern Fourier analysis of non-periodic signals.

    • One must refer to the convergence proof of the inverse Fourier transform instead (see the Signal decomposition section of The Fourier transform blog post).

Fourier series

  • Fourier’s assumption

    • For a periodic signal with period \(T\) (or frequency \(\xi = \frac{1}{T}\))

    • and with \(n\) an integer.

      \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}cos(2\pi n\xi t) + b_{n}sin(2\pi n\xi t) \right\rbrack\) (1)

  • As explained in the Representations of an oscillation section of the Fourier transform blog post, complex exponentials are easier to manipulate.

  • As shown in the From sine/cosine to complex exponentials section below, expression (1) above be rewritten as

    \(f(t) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi n\xi t}\) (2)

Fourier series coefficients

  • The From Fourier series intuition to Fourier series coefficients shows that

    \(c_{n} = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi n\xi t}dt\)

From the Fourier Series to the Fourier Transform

  • This section explains how the Fourier transform can then be derived from the Fourier series.

  • In expression (2) above, the infinite sum is discrete.

    • It is the sum over the integer multiples of \(\xi\).

    • All the terms in the sum are evenly spaced every \(\xi\).

    • Where \(\xi = \frac{1}{T}\) and \(T\) is the period of the periodic signal.

  • A non-periodic signal can be seen as the limit of a periodic signal as its period \(T \rightarrow \infty\).

    • As \(T \rightarrow \infty\),

    • \(\xi = \frac{1}{T} \rightarrow 0\).

    • Hence, the spacing between the terms in the sum tends toward \(0\),

    • and the sum becomes an integral.

    • This gives

      \(f(t) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}c_{\xi}e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi\)

  • If \(c_{\xi}\) is written as \(F(\xi)\) instead, this gives

    \(f(t) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi\) (3)

  • Which is the same as expression (2) in the Fourier transform blog post.

  • Note that even though expression (3) is the limit of the Fourier series (expression (2)) as \(T \rightarrow \infty\), it is not called the Fourier transform.

    • In fact, it is the inverse Fourier transform instead.

    • The Fourier transform is the function \(F(\xi)\) of \(\xi\) (i.e. the Fourier transform coefficients).

  • Hence, moving from periodic to non-periodic signals

    • Let \(T \rightarrow \infty\),

    • Moves from the Fourier series to the Fourier transform,

    • Moves from discrete frequencies to continuous frequencies,

    • Moves from sums to integrals.

From sine/cosine to complex exponentials

Fourier series intuition

  • \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}cos(2\pi n\xi t) + b_{n}sin(2\pi n\xi t) \right\rbrack\) (1)

Euler’s formula

  • \(e^{ix} = cos(x) + i\ sin(x)\)

  • Thus,

    • \(cos(x) = \frac{e^{ix} + \ e^{- ix}}{2}\)

    • \(sin(x) = \frac{e^{ix} - \ e^{- ix}}{2i}\)

Plug into Fourier series intuition

  • \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}cos(2\pi n\xi t) + b_{n}sin(2\pi n\xi t) \right\rbrack\) (1)

    \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}\frac{e^{i2\pi n\xi} + \ e^{- i2\pi n\xi}}{2} + b_{n}\frac{e^{i2\pi n\xi} - \ e^{- i2\pi n\xi}}{2i} \right\rbrack\)

    \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack (\frac{a_{n}}{2} + \frac{b_{n}}{2i})e^{i2\pi n\xi} + (\frac{a_{n}}{2} - \frac{b_{n}}{2i})e^{- i2\pi n\xi} \right\rbrack\)

    \(f(t) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi n\xi t}\) (2)

  • For

    \(c_{n} = (\frac{a_{n}}{2} + \frac{b_{n}}{2i})\) for \(n > 0\)

    \(c_{- n} = (\frac{a_{n}}{2} - \frac{b_{n}}{2i})\) for \(n > 0\)

    \(c_{0} = \frac{a_{0}}{2}\)

Conclusion

  • \(f(t) = \frac{a_{0}}{2} + \sum_{n = 1}^{\infty}\left\lbrack a_{n}cos(2\pi n\xi t) + b_{n}sin(2\pi n\xi t) \right\rbrack\) (1)

    is simplified to

    \(f(t) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi n\xi t}\) (2)

  • Complex exponentials appear.

  • Positive and negative frequencies appear.

From Fourier series intuition to Fourier series coefficients

  • \(f(t) = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi n\xi t}\) (2)

  • Multiply by \(e^{- i2\pi m\xi t}\) and take the integral on both sides

    \(\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi m\xi t}dt = \int_{0}^{T}\sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt\)

    \(\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi m\xi t}dt = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}\int_{0}^{T}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt\)

  • Observe that \(\int_{0}^{T}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt = T\) for \(n \neq m\) else \(0\) (orthogonality of the \(e^{i2\pi k\xi t}\) functions)

    • If \(n \neq m\)

      \(\int_{0}^{T}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt\)

      \(= \left\lbrack \frac{1}{i2\pi(n - m)\xi}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t} \right\rbrack_{0}^{T}\)

      \(= \frac{1}{i2\pi(n - m)\xi}(e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi T} - 1)\)

      \(= \frac{1}{i2\pi(n - m)\xi}(e^{i2\pi(n - m)} - 1)\) because \(\xi = \frac{1}{T}\)

      \(= \frac{1}{i2\pi(n - m)\xi}(1 - 1)\)

      \(= 0\)

    • if \(n = m\)

      \(\int_{0}^{T}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt\)

      \(= \int_{0}^{T}1\ dt\)

      \(= T\)

  • Thus,

    \(\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi m\xi t}dt = \sum_{n = - \infty}^{\infty}c_{n}\int_{0}^{T}e^{i2\pi(n - m)\xi t}dt = c_{m}\ T\)

    \(c_{m} = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi m\xi t}dt\)

From Fourier decomposition to Fourier transform coefficients

  • \(f(t) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi\) (3)

  • Multiply by \(e^{- i2\pi\nu t}\) and take the integral on both sides

    \(\int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi\nu t}dt = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}\left( \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi \right)e^{- i2\pi\nu t}dt\)

    \(\int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi\nu t}dt = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)\left( \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}e^{i2\pi(\xi - \nu)t}dt \right)d\xi\)

  • Observe that \(\int_{- \infty}^{\infty}e^{i2\pi(\xi - \nu)t}dt = \delta(\xi - \nu)\) (orthogonality of the \(e^{i2\pi\xi t}\) functions)

  • Thus,

    \(\int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi\nu t}dt = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)\left( \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}e^{i2\pi(\xi - \nu)t}dt \right)d\xi = F(\nu)\)

    \(F(\nu) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)\ e^{- i2\pi\nu t}dt\)
