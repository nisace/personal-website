Usage

  • The Fourier series and the Fourier transform allow the analysis of the content of a signal.

  • This analysis is referred to as the Fourier analysis.

  • More specifically, they decompose the signal into oscillations at different frequencies.

    • See the Representations of an oscillation section below to see how these oscillations can be represented.

  • This decomposition into oscillations at different frequencies gives information about the structure of the signal in terms of pitch, harmonics or timbre for example.

Differences between the Fourier series and the Fourier transform

  • The Fourier series applies to periodic signals.

  • The Fourier transform applies to non-periodic signals.

  • Both describe the signal as a sum (or integral) of oscillations at different frequencies.

  • The From the Fourier Series to the Fourier section in the blog post of the same name explains how the Fourier transform can be derived from the Fourier series.

Representations of an oscillation

  • At each frequency, the oscillation of frequency \(f\) can be represented in different ways.

Sine and cosine representation

  • Either as a linear combination of a sine function \(sin(2\pi\xi t)\) and a cosine function \(cos(2\pi\xi t)\).

    • Both functions are thus real valued.

Complex exponentials representation

  • Or as a linear combination of two complex exponentials \(e^{- i2\pi\xi t}\) and \(e^{i2\pi\xi t}\).

    • Both functions are thus complex valued.

  • These two representations are mathematically equivalent.

    • Historically, the sine and cosine functions were used first.

    • However, the complex exponentials simplify the notation and are easier to manipulate.

    • The From sine/cosine to complex exponentials section from the From the Fourier series to the Fourier transform blog post explains the relationship between these two representations.

  • In both representations, the linear combination accounts for the phase of the signal.

    • If an oscillation of frequency \(f\) is present in the signal, the relative weights given to \(sin(2\pi\xi t)\) and \(cos(2\pi\xi t)\) or \(e^{- i2\pi\xi t}\) and \(e^{i2\pi\xi t}\) allow to produce an oscillation of frequency \(f\) with the same phase as the oscillation of frequency \(f\) present in the signal.

  • The weights of the linear combinations at the different frequencies are called the coefficients (Fourier series coefficients and Fourier transform coefficients).

Modern Fourier analysis

  • Historically, the Fourier series were developed first by Joseph Fourier.

  • In modern Fourier analysis, the Fourier transform is most often used instead of the Fourier series.

  • The Fourier transform is a generalization of the Fourier series to non-periodic signals.

  • This section gives the definition of the Fourier transform coefficients.

    • It introduces the definition but does not explain where it comes from.

    • See the From the Fourier series to the Fourier transform blog post to see where the Fourier transform comes from.

Fourier Transform

Definition

  • The Fourier transform usually represents oscillations with complex exponentials.

  • The Fourier transform coefficients are thus complex numbers.

  • At frequency \(\xi\), the Fourier transform coefficient is computed as follows:

    \(F(\xi) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}f(t)e^{- i2\pi\xi t}dt\) (1)

  • The amplitude \(\left| F(\xi) \right|\) indicates how strongly the oscillation of frequency \(\xi\) is present in the signal.

  • The phase \(arg(F(\xi))\) indicates the phase of the oscillation of frequency \(\xi\) present in the signal.

Interpretation

  • \(F(\xi)\) is equal to

    • The correlation between \(f(t)\) and \(e^{i2\pi\xi t}\).

    • The \(L^{2}\) inner product between \(f(t)\) and \(e^{i2\pi\xi t}\).

    • See the Cross-correlation, Correlation, Convolution, Inner product and Fourier coefficients post for more details.

Signal decomposition

  • Any signal with finite energy (i.e. \(\in L^{2}(R)\)) can be expressed and decomposed as an infinite sum (integral) of oscillations at all frequencies.

    • The finite energy condition is always met in practice as real-world signals cannot have infinite energy.

  • Each oscillation of frequency \(\xi\) is weighted by its Fourier transform coefficient \(F(\xi)\).

    \(f(t) = \int_{- \infty}^{\infty}F(\xi)e^{i2\pi\xi t}d\xi\) (2)
